Rare 2016 Jefferson nickel sells for $123 online - do you have the coin in your money jar?

May 26, 2024
blog

IT'S possible your Jefferson nickels could be quite valuable, as one has sold for more than $120 online.

Since 1938, Jefferson nickels have been struck for circulation by the US Mint.

A key factor collectors are interested in when it comes to coins is low mintage.

The scarcest one in the Jefferson series is dated 1950 with a D letter mark, which stands for the mint location of Denver.

It has reported production of more than 2.6million.

While that isn't considered low, especially when compared to the Lincoln VDB penny - there are luckily other elements that collectors are attracted to.

One of them is errors, which happens when the mint incorrectly manufactures a piece.

Why is the coin valuable?

And that's exactly what happened with a Jefferson nickel that was minted in 2016.

It also features a P mark, which stands for Philadelphia.

Most notably, you can see the error on the obverse right by Jefferson's right ear.

This is described by the seller as a "strike through" error.

"Struck throughs occur when a piece of foreign material comes between the die and a planchet during striking," Numismatic Guaranty Company (NGC) wrote.

"The coin is said to be “struck through” the foreign material, which leaves behind an impression on the coin’s surface.

According to the seller, the strike on the 2016 nickel featured a 90-degree rotation.

Importantly, It's safe to say that the coin was in high demand amongst collectors, as users submitted a total of 23 bids for the error piece.

Eventually, it wound up selling for $122.50, which would mean the nickel is now worth 244,900% its denomination.

What other Jefferson nickels are valuable?

If you don't have that particular coin, there are other Jefferson pieces that can be worth in the hundreds as well.

These contain errors as well.

One of them is the Henning Jefferson nickel, as one recently sold for $425 online.

It features a crack on the lower part of the letter “R” in the word “PLURIBUS,” which can be found on the top of the reverse.

While these are counterfeit, coin collectors value them today due to their iconic history.

Another Jefferson error piece with an embedded copper error on the reverse sold for more than $300.

It was dated 1975 without a mintmark.

Moreover, another Jefferson nickel with a 1984 date sold for $150.

It contained a cud error at the bottom right of the obverse.
